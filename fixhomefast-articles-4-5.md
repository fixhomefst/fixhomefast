# How to Deep Clean Your Bathroom in 30 Minutes

[Image: A bright, sparkling clean bathroom with white tiles and no clutter]

Let's be honest — the bathroom is the room everyone avoids cleaning. But a dirty bathroom doesn't just look bad. It builds up bacteria, mold, and soap scum that become harder to remove the longer you wait.

The good news? You don't need an entire afternoon. With the right plan, you can do a proper deep clean in 30 minutes flat.

Here's exactly how to do it.

---

## What You'll Need Before You Start

Gather everything before you begin so you're not running around mid-clean. You'll need:

- An all-purpose cleaner or white vinegar spray
- Toilet bowl cleaner
- Scrub brush for the toilet
- Microfiber cloths or old rags (2–3)
- Sponge or scrubbing pad
- Mop or floor cloth
- Rubber gloves

**Pro tip:** Pre-spray surfaces as you walk in and let the cleaner sit while you work on other areas. That dwell time does most of the scrubbing for you.

---

## The 30-Minute Bathroom Deep Clean Routine

### Minutes 0–3: Spray Everything First

As soon as you walk in, spray the toilet (inside the bowl with toilet cleaner, outside surfaces with all-purpose), the sink, the shower/tub walls, and the mirror. Let everything sit and soak while you work elsewhere. This is the secret to cleaning fast — you're not waiting for products, they're working while you move.

### Minutes 3–8: Tackle the Shower and Tub

Scrub the shower walls starting from the top and working down. Focus on grout lines, the corners, and the floor of the tub — these collect the most grime. Rinse thoroughly.

If there's soap scum on glass doors, a paste of baking soda and dish soap applied with a sponge and then rinsed off works better than most commercial sprays.

[Image: Hand scrubbing tile grout in a shower with a brush]

> **💡 Tip:** For tough limescale on showerheads, soak a cloth in white vinegar, wrap it around the showerhead, and leave it for 10 minutes. It dissolves mineral buildup without scrubbing.

### Minutes 8–14: Clean the Toilet

By now your toilet cleaner has been sitting for several minutes. Scrub the inside of the bowl with a toilet brush, focusing under the rim where bacteria hide. Then wipe down the outside — lid, seat (both sides), base, and the area behind the toilet where dust and hair collect.

> **⚠️ Warning:** Never mix bleach-based cleaners with ammonia or vinegar. The combination produces toxic fumes. Use one product at a time.

### Minutes 14–20: Sink and Faucet

Scrub the sink basin with a sponge, paying attention to where the drain meets the porcelain — that ring of buildup is mostly soap residue and is easy to remove with a little scrubbing. Wipe down the faucet handles and the counter surface.

To remove white mineral deposits around the faucet base, dab on a bit of white vinegar and let it sit for 2 minutes before wiping off.

### Minutes 20–24: Mirror and Surfaces

Spray the mirror with glass cleaner or a 50/50 mix of white vinegar and water. Wipe in a circular motion, then buff dry with a clean cloth. Wipe down any shelves, the top of the toilet tank, and any soap dispensers or accessories.

### Minutes 24–28: Mop the Floor

Spray or pour your floor cleaner and mop the floor, starting from the far corner and working toward the door. Don't forget behind the toilet and around the base of the toilet — these are often the grimiest spots. Let the floor dry before walking on it.

### Minutes 28–30: Final Touches

Empty the trash bin. Replace toilet paper if needed. Fold the hand towel neatly. Wipe any water spots you may have dripped. Step back and you're done.

---

## How to Keep It Clean Between Deep Cleans

A 30-minute deep clean works best if you maintain it between sessions. Small daily habits make a huge difference:

- **Wipe down the sink** after brushing your teeth
- **Squeegee the shower walls** after each use (reduces soap scum and mold significantly)
- **Keep a toilet brush accessible** for quick weekly scrubs
- **Run the exhaust fan** during and after showers to reduce moisture and mold growth

If you do these four things consistently, your 30-minute deep clean might only be needed once a month.

## Quick Recap: 30-Minute Bathroom Cleaning Plan

1. **0–3 min** – Pre-spray all surfaces
2. **3–8 min** – Scrub shower and tub
3. **8–14 min** – Clean toilet inside and out
4. **14–20 min** – Scrub sink and faucet
5. **20–24 min** – Mirror and shelf surfaces
6. **24–28 min** – Mop the floor
7. **28–30 min** – Trash, towels, final wipe

Thirty minutes. Every week. That's all it takes to have a bathroom that's always company-ready.

# Why Is My Gas Burner Not Lighting? Easy Fixes

[Image: Close-up of a gas stove burner cap being cleaned with a toothbrush]

You turn the knob, hear the clicking — but nothing lights. It's one of the most frustrating kitchen problems, especially when you're in the middle of cooking. The good news is that in most cases, this is a simple fix you can do yourself in under 15 minutes.

Here's why it happens and exactly how to fix it.

---

## Most Common Reasons a Gas Burner Won't Light

Before you panic or call a technician, it helps to know the usual suspects:

1. **Clogged burner ports** – Food debris and spills block the tiny holes gas flows through
2. **Wet or dirty igniter** – The igniter spark plug gets coated with grease or moisture
3. **Misaligned burner cap** – The cap that sits on top isn't seated properly
4. **Faulty igniter** – Less common, but the igniter itself may be worn out
5. **Gas supply issue** – Rarely, but worth checking

Let's go through each fix from simplest to more involved.

---

## Fix 1: Clean the Burner Ports (Most Common Fix)

This solves the problem about 70% of the time. Food debris, boiled-over liquids, and grease accumulate inside the small holes around the burner head and block gas flow.

**How to clean them:**

1. Make sure the stove is completely off and cool
2. Remove the burner grate (the metal rack that holds pots)
3. Lift off the burner cap (the flat disc sitting on top)
4. Look at the burner head — you'll see small holes around the perimeter
5. Use a toothpick, straightened paper clip, or a thin wire to clear each hole
6. Rinse the burner cap and head under warm water, scrub with an old toothbrush and dish soap
7. Dry completely before reassembling — even a little moisture prevents ignition

> **⚠️ Warning:** Never use a toothpick or wooden skewer and leave it inside the hole. It can break off and make the blockage worse. Use metal or remove it carefully.

[Image: Burner head with clogged ports next to a cleaned one for comparison]

---

## Fix 2: Dry Out a Wet Igniter

If you recently cleaned your stove — or if liquid boiled over onto the burner — the igniter (that small white ceramic nub near the burner) may be wet. A wet igniter sparks but won't catch gas.

**How to fix it:**

- Turn on all the burners briefly to help dry out the igniter through heat and airflow
- Use a hair dryer on low to gently dry the igniter area for 1–2 minutes
- Let the stove sit for 30 minutes before trying again

This is especially common after heavy rain (gas line humidity) or after cleaning the stovetop with too much water.

---

## Fix 3: Reseat the Burner Cap

The burner cap is the flat disc that sits directly on top of the burner head. It needs to be perfectly flat and centered for gas to distribute evenly and light properly.

**To check and fix:**

1. Remove the cap completely
2. Place it back on — it should sit flat with no wobble
3. Make sure it's aligned with any notch or groove on the burner head
4. Try lighting again

This is a surprisingly common cause that people overlook. If the cap is even slightly tilted, the gas path gets disrupted.

---

## Fix 4: Clean the Igniter Tip

If the igniter is coated with grease or has residue on it, it won't spark properly.

- With the stove off, locate the small white ceramic igniter tip near the burner
- Use a cotton swab dipped lightly in rubbing alcohol to wipe it clean
- Let it dry completely before testing

> **💡 Tip:** Be gentle — igniters are fragile. Don't scrub with metal or anything abrasive.

---

## Fix 5: Check the Gas Supply

If none of the above works, verify your gas supply isn't the issue:

- Check if other burners light — if they all fail, there may be a gas supply interruption
- Make sure the gas valve behind or under the stove is fully open (parallel to the pipe = open)
- If you use a gas cylinder, check if it needs replacing

> **⚠️ If you smell gas** and the burner won't light, do NOT keep clicking the igniter. Turn off the knob, ventilate the room immediately by opening windows, and leave the house. Call your gas supplier.

---

## When to Call a Technician

Most of the time, the fixes above solve the problem. But call a professional if:

- The igniter sparks constantly even when you're not turning the knob
- There's visible damage to the igniter or burner wiring
- The burner lights but the flame is uneven, orange/yellow instead of blue, or very weak after cleaning
- You smell gas consistently even with everything off
